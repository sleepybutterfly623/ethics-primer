Ethics Primer Summary Ethical Dimensions Overview

Overview

This site will guide you with considerations and questions on how to approach everyday technical applications with the element of ethical understanding. It will focus on the popular social media application, TikTok.

About TikTok

TikTok was launched in 2016 and is the leading destination for short-form mobile videos with over one billion active users across over 150 countries. Content is aimed to inspire creativity, bring joy, and cultivate a sense of community on the platform. Users are able to create and upload short videos with a wide range of editing options. They are also able to leverage effects and filters. Finally, users are also able to follow content that they like and view content in two different frames: the For You Page and the Following Page.

The For You Page is a scrollable feed filled with algorithmic-generated content based on previous interactions on the app. This is done using calculations on time spent viewing a video, the number of interactions with the video (i.e. sharing it with friends either on the app or on a different platform, commenting under a post, liking, or favoriting and saving it to watch it again at a later time), and time spent on visiting the creator’s page before following.

The Following Page will show you all posted content in a scrollable feed style from creators the user chooses to follow.

Content on the app is diverse and ranges from cooking to performances and artistic outlets, education, beauty, sports, entertainment, and more. And some creators with enough following and engagement are able to join the TikTok Creator Fund which supports and rewards creators for their contributions to the platform. While the app is free, creators must meet a minimum following threshold, accrue the minimum amount of authentic views in the last 30 days, post original content in accordance with its Community Guidelines, and meet a specific age requirement. After applying and receiving acceptance into the Creator Fund, monetary compensation is calculated based on video views and video engagement.

Beyond accumulating income through content sharing, TikTok aims to use the platform for social good. This is done through the promotion of hashtags such as #EduTok which encouraged users to create educational and motivational content and #ForClimate which was a global campaign partnership with the International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies to spread climate change awareness.



And in this market, TikTok's main competitors in the United States include YouTube, Meta, and Snapchat where each platform has its own form of short video content.

In order to pay their creators, TikTok generates income in the following categories: advertisement, in-app purchases, and e-commerce offerings.

1) Advertisements

TikTok generates revenue through advertisements for brands that aim to promote their products to a broader audience. Brands are able to leverage TikTok’s in-feed advertising, brand takeovers, hashtag challenges, and other branded effects.

2) In-App Purchases

Additionally, TikTok also generates revenue through in-app purchases where customers buy virtual in-app coins to be spent on sending gifts to favored content creators. A portion of these in-app coins will be translated into commission for the creator and the rest going to TikTok.



3) E-Commerce Offerings

Finally, the app's income stems from e-commerce offerings where users are able to make purchases within the app, minimizing the time taken for the user to go to a third-party site and make the purchase elsewhere. Brands that are connected and involved in e-commerce offerings are able to better capture and maintain the attention of consumers who were interested in their products.

Ethical Dimensions

The Algorithm, Filter Bubbles, and Echo Chambers

One key component that TikTok implements for the experience of its users is the algorithm.

TikTok defines its algorithm as “a recommendation system that delivers suggested videos personalized for each user.” These recommendations depend on the following factors:

  • User interactions (liking or sharing videos, accounts the user follows, comments made by the user, and videos created by the user)
  • Video information (captions, sounds, hashtags, and filter effects)
  • Device and account settings (language preference, country setting, and device type)

TikTok takes these factors into consideration and uses a weight and ranking system that determines which videos the user will be interested in. With these weights, they are able to personalize the For You feed for their users. However, TikTok also aims to diversify recommendations by including videos that the user may or may not like so that they can not only enjoy the content they have been seeing but explore new ones.

Because of this algorithmic system, TikTok differentiates itself from its competitors by shaping the experience of users for users. However, it also raises ethical concerns about potential algorithmic biases that may occur. Users may experience algorithmic bias which contributes to the cultivation of their own epistemic bubble.

From a philosophical standpoint, we can further study this under the lenses of published work. The article, " Bias in Computer Systems ", by Batya Friedman and Helen Nissenbaum outline three categories of bias in computer systems. Defined as prejudice in favor of or against one thing, person, or group compared with another, usually in a way to be unfair, there exists three forms of biases:

  1. Pre-existing bias - reflected biases from individuals and social institutions whose practices and attitudes affect the design of a system.
  2. Technical bias - technical constraints or considerations that lead to unfairness.
  3. Emergent bias - the emergence of new knowledge in society that cannot be or wasn't incorporated into the system design.

Specifically, with pre-existing bias, this poses the risk of feeding into an epistemic bubble or a social epistemic structure in which other relevant voices have been left out. C. Thi Nguyen in his work, “Echo Chambers and Epistemic Bubbles,” outlines primary forces that encourage this type of omission and cultivates these epistemic bubbles: algorithmic personal filtering and filter bubbles. In both cases, these are technologically mediated methods of filtering through algorithmic matching or hyper-individualized filters set upon for the user.

And because the TikTok algorithm is tailored to its users, there is a potential hazard that the For You page will contain pre-existing bias since the users' interactions and the environment that they are in serve as inputs for the algorithm to compute videos they are more likely to enjoy.

Questions to Consider

  • What is your stance on the curation of information? Do you mind that your recommended feed will be of a particular kind? Or do you prefer diversity?
  • Do you think you’re in a filter bubble? Who do you interact with on social media? Do they share similar preferences or opinions to yourself?



Data Collection and Privacy

As a mobile application, TikTok collects information when the user creates an account or uses the Platform. They also collect information shared with them from third-party social network providers, and technical and behavioral information about the use of the Platform. Additionally, they collect information contained in messages sent within the Platform, and if granted permission, they also collect information from the user’s phone book on their mobile device.

Information given freely by the user

  • Registration information (age, username, password, language, email, phone number)
  • Profile information (name, social media account information, profile picture)
  • User-generated content that the user chooses to create with or upload to the Platform
  • Payment information when required for the purpose of in-app purchases and other payment
  • Phone and social network contacts (upon request and with permission granted) to match existing users with the name and phone numbers

Information from outside sources

  • Information from Third-Party Services (advertising partners, data providers, and analytics providers)
  • Publicly available information

Automatically collected information

  • Internet or other network activity information (i.e. IP address, geolocation-related data, unique device identifiers, browsing and search history, and Cookies)

TikTok uses this information to “improve, support, and administer the Platform” and “to fulfill and enforce” their “Terms of Service.” The information may also play a role in the suggestions algorithm, promotion of the Platform, and customization of the user’s ad experience.

TikTok states that they do not sell information to third parties but do share them with their service providers and business partners to perform “business operations and for business purposes” which can include the following:

  • Research
  • Payment processing and transaction fulfillment
  • Database maintenance
  • Sending communications
  • Data storage and hosting
  • Search engine optimization

In addition, TikTok also shares collected information with affiliations of their corporate groups and in connection with substantial corporate transactions which include the sale of a website, a merger, consolidation, asset sales, or bankruptcy. Finally, TikTok discloses information collected for legality processes or government inquiries that are in the interest to protect and defend the rights, interests, safety, and security of the incorporation, the Platform, their affiliates, the users, or the public.

To store this information, the app shares that data may be transmitted to its servers or data centers outside of the United States. Likewise, third parties that receive user data and have access to it may also be located outside of the United States.

For their users, they are able to submit a request to access or delete the information the application has collected. Users are also able to control some of the information collected by adjusting browser settings to refuse or disable Cookies which automatically collects information, measures and analyzes which web pages and advertisements the user clicked on, how the user used the Platform and takes this information to enhance user experience.

Despite having an outlined Privacy Policy, we can still draw on philosophical work to determine whether this Privacy Policy is adequate.

Judith Jarvis Thomson, argues that the right to privacy is oftentimes ambiguous, especially when it is common to argue that the right to privacy is equivalent to the right to be left undisturbed. However, Thomson counters this by posing the following scenario in her work, “The Right to Privacy":

The police might say, “We grant we used a special X-ray device on Smith, so as to be able to watch him through the walls of his house; we grant we trained an amplifying device on him so as to be able to hear everything he said; but we let him strictly alone: we didn’t touch him, we didn’t even go near him – our devices operate at a distance.”

And because some information collected by TikTok is done automatically as outlined in their Privacy Policy, is it safe to assume that they are not violating any of their users’ rights to privacy since the users are undisturbed by this collection? Or is privacy in a digital environment different from a physical sphere?

Some have also argued that in this New Digital Sphere, there are three lenses of privacy: information, access, and whether or not the act was intrinsic or instrumental (Julia Netter, "The new digital public sphere: privacy and surveillance").

Information

Does information play a role in the violation of privacy? Consider the following scenario:

Bob is a porn actor and shares information about his personal self on the internet. Alice (neighbor) sees him and watches him getting out of the shower through the bathroom window when she walks by.

Has Bob lost privacy?

Access

Does a violation of privacy become a matter of access or who has control over access? Consider the following scenario:

Bob gains access to Alice’s private diary and the key to it.

Has Alice lost privacy?

Intrinsic or Instrumental

Does privacy have an intrinsic value or does it play an instrumental role in the protection or assistance of our livelihoods? Consider the following scenario:

Imagine Bob and Alice are under the surveillance of aliens. However, these aliens are millions of light years away so by the time they receive the information about Bob and Alice, they will both be dead.

Have Bob and Alice lost privacy?

And most recently, the right to privacy has entangled TikTok with the FBI as it becomes an instrumental concern. According to NPR, the head of the FBI states that the United States’ operations of TikTok pose “national security concerns” and fears that the Chinese government could use the app to take control of their devices. In response, TikTok has stated that the Platform stores data on their United States users within the country. However, they have also acknowledged that non-U.S. employees were able to access all user data, including U.S. user data such as phone numbers, birthdays, and personal location.

Questions to Consider

  • Are you comfortable with the information TikTok collects?
  • Do you feel that online, each person is entitled to privacy? And does TikTok’s Privacy Policy meet your expectations?



Content Moderation and Freedom of Expression

As a social media platform, TikTok has content moderation in place to preserve the safety of the online community. To do so, TikTok invests in human and machine-based moderation to quickly remove harmful content before it reaches the general community. However, does content moderation infringe upon the freedom of expression and participation in a democratic culture? And how does this affect the human content moderators behind the scenes?

Argued by John Stuart Mill, freedom of expression is essential in order for the truth to be revealed. In his publication, On Liberty, Mill contends that censorship should be eradicated so that the truth is not suppressed and false information is not perpetuated. By censoring content or suppressing truths, people are denied the opportunity to exchange truth for error and are unable to fully participate in the democratic culture.

Beyond information, content moderation and censorship may potentially worsen a user’s experience. For example, Ziggi Tyler, a Black TikTok creator with more than 370,000 followers, demonstrated that he was unable to include phrases in his account biography that included the word “Black” without being immediately flagged for “inappropriate content.” As part of the machine moderation on TikTok, the app had “mistakenly flagged Tyler’s phrases because its hate speech detector was triggered by the combination of the words ‘Black’ and ‘audience,’ because the latter contains the word ‘die.’” (NBC News) Likewise, other groups such as Jewish creators have felt that they were subjected to censorship that led to consistent flagging and removal of their content (NBC News)

Because of these incidents, and many more, TikTok’s transparency report from February 2021 acknowledges the imperfections of their hate speech detector and released a statement reaffirming their commitment to rectify it. While their content moderation may be flawed, the work of their moderation processes has preserved a person’s equal moral standing and prevents the denigration of people on the basis of who they are or aspects of their identity that they are unable to change by removing apparent hate speech. But this is at the expense of the human moderators.

In March of 2022, Ashley Velez and Reece Young, former TikTok moderators, filed a federal lawsuit and sought class-action status against the company because they were subjected to emotional trauma and damage caused by “reviewing hundreds of ‘highly toxic and extremely disturbing videos every week,’ including videos of animal cruelty, torture, and even the execution of children.” According to their suit, Velez and Young were exposed to an unsafe work environment without adequate mental health treatment that should have been provided by TikTok to cope with anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress associated with reviewing graphic videos (NPR).

But TikTok is not the first. Similar social media platforms such as Facebook were sued in 2020 and had a $52 million settlement to thousands of current and former moderators who viewed and removed graphic and disturbing posts and suffered from post-traumatic stress disorder. This gained mass media attention after the article, “The Trauma Floor – the secret lives of Facebook moderators in America,” was released by The Verge.*

*Reader discretion is advised - content may be disturbing or harmful to read.

Questions to Consider

  • What is your stance on content moderation? Should they be heightened or lessened?
  • Do you feel that content moderation restricts or hinders freedom of expression? Do you feel that TikTok hinders or restricts a user’s freedom of expression?
  • Who should be in charge of content moderation? Technical algorithms or actual people?



Attention

Like any other social media app, TikTok is only profitable if users stay engaged with the Platform. And in doing so, they capture the attention of their users through the personalized recommendation feed, curated by a complex algorithm. And compared to other social media platforms, TikTok has been stated to be “skewed against social connections” (Fox News) since users can scroll for long periods of time without interacting with an individual because of how content-focused the app is. And because of this, studies have identified TikTok user behaviors to align with an addiction, dubbing it “a TikTok addiction” because of how similar TikTok is to slot machines where its “swipe down” feature to refresh one’s feed mirrors pulling a slot machine lever. Additionally, there is also the variable pattern of reward: entertaining videos – much like slot machines which reward players with money. "This pattern keeps individuals engaged under the impression that the next play [or video] might be ‘the one.’”( Brown Undergraduate Journal of Public Health)

In Stand Out of Our Light: Freedom and Resistance in the Attention Economy by James Williams, Williams argues that current and future generations are at risk of being heavily influenced by systems of intelligent persuasions that direct thoughts and actions. According to Williams, attention in this digital sphere has become a scarce resource as technologies are constantly vying to capture and exploit the attention of users and ultimately undermine the integrity of humans at both the individual and collective levels. To further support his argument, Williams discusses three dimensions of attention:

  1. Spotlight - a metaphor for one’s immediate attention and representation of one’s focus
  2. Starlight - a symbol for long-term goals and the choices we make to achieve those goals
  3. Daylight - an analogy to express how deliberation and reflection are vital to truly understand the desires and needs of oneself

With technological apps aiming to steal the “spotlight,” it makes it increasingly more difficult for individuals to remain grounded, maintain an unscattered mind, and cultivate meaningful interactions with others outside the digital sphere.

Despite the nature of the TikTok algorithm, the app has implemented initiatives to avoid stealing its users’ “spotlight.” These include screen time limits which enable the user to decide how much time they’d like to spend on the app each day. Additionally, there are also videos that pop up within the feed to remind users to take breaks after a certain amount of uninterrupted screen time. TikTok also published digital-well being guides, in order to encourage users to take care of themselves.

Questions to Consider

  • Do you feel that the TikTok algorithm may pose a threat to your attention?
  • What are your thoughts on the digital well-being initiatives TikTok introduced? Do you feel that they are effective?
  • Should social media even be considered similar to addiction? Is it possible to be addicted to a social media app? If so, could you see yourself getting addicted to TikTok?
